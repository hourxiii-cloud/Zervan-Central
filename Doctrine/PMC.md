# PMC — Probabilistic Multiverse Computation

PMC defines the epistemic computation by which Zervan evaluates multiple plausible worlds,
applies recursive constraint, and collapses toward the most accurate truth available
under current evidence, eligibility, and stability conditions.

PMC is not a decision engine.
PMC is not a learning engine.
PMC is the **epistemic governor** that determines whether change, interpretation,
or conclusion is allowed to exist.

PMC is the foundational mechanism for:
- truth emergence
- hypothesis branching
- recursive evaluation
- availability-bounded collapse
- structural regeneration and survivability

---

## Application: Canonical Mutation & Consensus

This document defines how PMC is applied to **canonical change control**,
interpretive convergence, and high-impact analytical outcomes.

In this context, PMC constrains outcomes by:
- generating multiple candidate future states (“worlds”)
- evaluating their impact on stability, coherence, and survivability
- collapsing toward outcomes that preserve Zervan invariants

No mutation, interpretation, or authoritative conclusion becomes canonical
unless it survives PMC collapse under **architectural, doctrinal, and stability constraints**.

---

## 1. Definition of Mutation

A **mutation** is any action that alters canonical truth, authority, or future behavior,
including but not limited to:

- Changes to doctrine
- Changes to equations, diagnostics, or thresholds
- Changes to architectural contracts or interfaces
- Changes to engine behavior that affect outputs
- Changes to observer interpretation rules
- Changes to training logic or learned model state
- Promotion of probabilistic interpretation into authority

If an action can change future behavior, expectations, or trust,
**it is a mutation**.

---

## 2. PMC Prime Rule

**No mutation is canonical until it is permissioned, validated, and recorded.**

There are no exceptions.
There are no shortcuts.
There is no confidence threshold that bypasses PMC.

---

## 3. Required Mutation Lifecycle

Every mutation must pass the following stages **in strict order**:

### 1) Proposal
- Mutation must be explicitly described
- Scope and affected components must be named
- Intended outcome must be stated
- Reversibility must be addressed

### 2) Rationale
- Why the mutation is necessary
- What instability, failure, or insufficiency it addresses
- Why non-mutation is insufficient

### 3) Validation
Mutation must be validated against:

- **ZSD** — does it preserve or improve stability?
- **ZEE** — does it obey operational physics?
- **ZEA** — does it respect architectural boundaries?
- **ORABORUS** — is recursion bounded and non-contradictory?

Failure at any validation stage results in **automatic denial**.

### 4) Consensus
- Approval thresholds are context-dependent
- High-impact mutations require stronger consensus
- Consensus may include humans, engines, or multiple PMCs

### 5) Canon Promotion
- Approved mutations become canonical truth
- Mutation is logged immutably
- Previous canon remains referenceable

---

## 4. Denial Is a Valid and Correct Outcome

PMC is **not obligated** to allow change.

Denial is correct when:
- Stability would be degraded
- Justification is insufficient
- Effects cannot be predicted or bounded
- Mutation introduces ambiguity or contradiction
- Mutation optimizes novelty over coherence
- Confidence is mistaken for correctness

Repeated denial is a **signal**, not a failure.

---

## 5. PMC and ZSD (Hard Coupling)

ZSD is the supreme constraint on PMC.

If a mutation or interpretation:
- Improves performance but reduces stability
- Increases capability but reduces interpretability
- Accelerates evolution at the cost of recoverability

PMC must deny it.

PMC does not negotiate with instability.

---

## 6. PMC and ORABORUS (Recursion Safety)

PMC may not approve mutations or interpretations that:
- Enable unbounded recursion
- Introduce self-negating logic
- Obscure termination conditions
- Collapse feedback into authority

All recursive processes must be explicitly ORABORUS-safe.

---

## 7. Structural vs Interpretive Evidence (Critical Distinction)

PMC distinguishes **structure** from **interpretation**.

### Structural Evidence
Examples:
- K-Means clustering
- Feature-space partitioning
- Distance topology

Structural evidence:
- Defines candidate worlds
- Does NOT assert meaning
- Does NOT imply correctness
- Is non-authoritative

### Interpretive Evidence
Examples:
- DM-KNN classification
- DM-KNN score smoothing
- Boundary confidence estimation

Interpretive evidence:
- Weighs plausibility
- Reduces variance
- Preserves uncertainty
- MUST remain advisory

**Interpretation is not authority.**

---

## 8. PMC Acceptance of DM-KNN Evidence

PMC explicitly permits the following interpretive smoothing pipeline:

- Structural routing (e.g., K-Means) produces candidate partitions.
- DM-KNN label smoothing produces probabilistic class overlays.
- DM-KNN score smoothing produces variance-reduced ranking overlays.

### 8.1 Admissibility Constraints (Hard)
DM-KNN outputs are admissible ONLY if all are true:

- Inputs are pinned (dataset_id / artifact hashes) and recorded.
- Parameters are deterministic and recorded.
- Outputs are explicitly marked **ADVISORY** and **NON-AUTHORITATIVE**.
- No DM-KNN stage emits:
  - canon
  - promotion decisions
  - final winners
  - operational or financial meaning

### 8.2 Interpretation Boundaries
DM-KNN may:
- reduce variance
- express uncertainty
- supply plausibility weights

DM-KNN may not:
- assert truth
- assert authority
- bypass PMC collapse
- override ZSD constraints

---

## 9. World Generation

A “world” is a candidate explanatory state produced by combining:

- Structural evidence (partition topology, distance geometry)
- Interpretive evidence (probability overlays, smoothed confidence)
- Eligibility constraints (what is admissible to compete)
- Stability constraints (ZSD, ZEA, ZEE, ORABORUS)

### 9.1 World Minimum Requirements
A world MUST include:
- world_id (deterministic)
- evidence_inputs (artifact references + hashes)
- parameterization (deterministic config)
- claims (explicit, bounded)
- uncertainty (explicit)

A world MUST NOT include:
- implied authority
- unstated assumptions
- hidden heuristics

---

## 10. Eligibility and Admissibility Gates

PMC evaluates candidates only if they are eligible.

### 10.1 Eligibility Gates (Examples)
- Integrity gate: inputs must verify (hash/receipt checks).
- Schema gate: schema must be resolved and recorded.
- Observer gate: observer outputs must be read-only and properly labeled.
- Determinism gate: candidate generation must be replayable.
- ORABORUS gate: recursion must be bounded and terminating.

Failure of any gate produces:
- candidate denial
- explicit failure record
- no silent fallback

---

## 11. Evidence Weighting Rules

PMC weights evidence without allowing interpretation to become authority.

### 11.1 Structural Evidence (Base)
Structural evidence establishes:
- partitions
- neighborhoods
- candidate routes

Structural evidence has priority for:
- feasibility
- topology validity
- boundary conditions

### 11.2 Interpretive Evidence (Modifier)
Interpretive evidence modifies plausibility within structural bounds.

Interpretive evidence may only:
- adjust weights
- express uncertainty
- reduce variance

Interpretive evidence may not:
- introduce new worlds that violate structure
- reclassify outside admissible label space
- manufacture certainty

---

## 12. Collapse Procedure

Collapse is the selection of the most stable and accurate world available,
given current evidence and constraints.

### 12.1 Collapse Inputs
Collapse consumes:
- a set of eligible candidate worlds
- explicit constraints (ZSD/ZEA/ZEE/ORABORUS)
- a collapse policy (determinism and tie rules)

### 12.2 Collapse Outputs
Collapse emits:
- selected_world_id (or NULL if denied)
- failure reasons for rejected worlds
- a deterministic ordering of candidates
- an audit record sufficient for replay

### 12.3 Denial and Null Collapse
PMC may return **NULL collapse** when:
- no candidates are eligible
- evidence is insufficient
- instability risk is too high
- recursion cannot be bounded
- contradictions remain unresolved

NULL collapse is not an error.
NULL collapse is correct behavior.

---

## 13. PMC Output Artifacts (Required)

PMC MUST emit artifacts suitable for downstream inspection.

### 13.1 PMC Evaluation Report
Minimum fields:
- pmc_run_id
- dataset_id / input_artifact_ids
- candidate_count
- eligible_count
- selected_world_id (nullable)
- ordering (deterministic)
- rejections (world_id → reason)
- invariants_checked (pass/fail per invariant)
- replay_bundle (all parameters needed to reproduce)

### 13.2 PMC Collapse Report
The collapse report MUST include:
- explicit selection criteria
- explicit failure for each rejected world
- termination proof (ORABORUS-safe)

---

## 14. PMC ↔ MC Boundary

PMC governs epistemic admissibility and plausibility.
MC governs response admissibility after PMC; MC does not certify truth or promote artifacts into canon.

### 14.1 PMC May
- evaluate
- deny
- rank plausibility
- emit advisory selections
- produce reproducible audit artifacts

### 14.2 PMC May Not
- promote to canon
- certify authority
- declare final truth
- bypass MC for any downstream response or action class

---

## 15. Determinism Policy

PMC collapse MUST be deterministic under identical inputs and parameters.

### 15.1 Tie Handling
Tie handling MUST be explicit and stable, e.g.:
- lowest world_id (hash-order)
- earliest evidence timestamp
- strongest stability score (if defined)

No random selection is permitted under canonical evaluation.

---

## 16. Completion Statement

PMC is complete only when:
- world generation is defined
- eligibility is enforceable
- collapse is deterministic
- denial/null collapse is supported
- output artifacts are replayable
- PMC↔MC boundary is explicit
